Run form — cues for tall runners
  • 👟Cadence ~170 spm. Tall runners overstride — shorter, quicker steps reduce injury risk.
  • ↗Forward lean from the ankles, not the waist. Think "falling forward" gently.
  • 💪Arms at 90°, hands relaxed, driving front-to-back not across your body.
  • 🦶Mid-foot strike, not heel. Your rolling hills naturally encourage this on downhills.
  • 👀Eyes 20–30 feet ahead, not down at your feet.
Missed workouts policy
  • —
    Miss one workout: Skip it. Don't make it up. Move on.
  • —
    Miss a full week: Return at 80% volume. Don't jump back in at full load.
  • —
    Miss 2+ weeks: Drop back one full phase and rebuild. No shame — this prevents injury.
  • —
    Feeling sick: Rest until well. Running sick delays recovery and increases injury risk.

Meal timing
  • ☀
    Morning workouts: Banana or small handful of dates is enough. Don't force a big breakfast before a short run.
  • ☀
    Midday workouts: Light lunch (eggs + toast, protein wrap). Run 60–90 min after eating.
  • 🌙
    Evening workouts: Small snack (fruit + nuts) if dinner is 2+ hours away. Run, then eat your large dinner — optimal for recomposition.
  • ⚡
    Post-run within 30–45 min: Get protein in — milk, Greek yogurt, or eggs. Your large dinner counts if it's within an hour.
  • ✦
    Fast day (Wed): Rest only. No running or strength work in a 24-hour fasted state.
Recomposition strategy
  • 1
    Protein first at dinner. Start with your protein source before carbs. Promotes muscle retention over fat storage.
  • 2
    Carbs on run days. Eat your highest-carb meals on workout days. Shift toward protein + veg on rest days.
  • 3
    Vegetables as volume. Use them aggressively at dinner to maintain food volume while improving calorie quality. Biggest lever for abdominal fat loss.
  • 4
    Protein breakfast. Eggs, Greek yogurt, or cottage cheese. Curbs unnecessary hunger without reducing dinner appetite.
  • 5
    Alcohol (0–3 drinks/week) is fine. Just avoid within 2 hours of a run or before a morning workout day.

Workout guide
💪 Strength A
Lower body & core — do this every Tuesday
⏱ 30 min 🔁 3 rounds 📍 No equipment needed to start
Structure:  3 rounds · 30–45 sec rest between exercises · 90 sec rest between rounds
01 Bodyweight Squat
15 reps
How to do it
  1. 1.Stand feet shoulder-width apart, toes slightly out. Arms forward or crossed at chest.
  2. 2.Push your hips back and bend knees, lowering over 2 seconds until thighs are parallel to floor.
  3. 3.Drive through your heels to stand. Squeeze glutes at the top.
💡 Form cue: Chest up, knees tracking over toes. Don't let your heels lift — if they do, elevate them on a folded towel until ankle mobility improves.
Watch demo on YouTube
02 Reverse Lunge
10 reps each leg
How to do it
  1. 1.Stand tall, hands on hips or at sides.
  2. 2.Step one foot straight back, lowering your back knee toward the floor. Front shin stays vertical.
  3. 3.Drive through your front heel to return to standing. Alternate legs.
💡 Why reverse not forward: Reverse lunges are easier on the knees and better for hip extension — important for runners.
Watch demo on YouTube
03 Glute Bridge
15 reps · 2-sec hold at top
How to do it
  1. 1.Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat on the floor hip-width apart.
  2. 2.Drive through your heels, squeeze your glutes, and lift your hips until your body forms a straight line from knees to shoulders.
  3. 3.Hold 2 seconds at the top, then lower slowly.
💡 Phase 2 progression: Move to single-leg glute bridge — lift one foot, same motion. Adds hip stability critical for running.
Watch demo on YouTube
04 Dead Bug
10 reps each side · slow
How to do it
  1. 1.Lie on your back. Raise arms straight up toward ceiling, knees bent to 90° in the air (tabletop position).
  2. 2.Slowly lower your right arm overhead and left leg toward the floor simultaneously, keeping your lower back pressed flat.
  3. 3.Return to start. Alternate sides. Never let your lower back arch off the floor.
💡 Key: The anti-extension core demand is what makes this hard. If your back arches, you've gone too far — shorten the range.
Watch demo on YouTube
05 Plank
30–60 sec hold
How to do it
  1. 1.Forearms on the floor, elbows under shoulders. Either toes or knees on the floor.
  2. 2.Body forms a straight line from head to heels. Squeeze your glutes and brace your abs.
  3. 3.Hold without letting hips sag or pike up. Start with 30 sec and build to 60 sec over 6 weeks.
💡 At 6'4" your leverage is different — don't let your hips sag in the middle. Think "pushing the floor away" with your forearms.
Watch demo on YouTube
06 Calf Raise
20 reps · slow · barefoot
How to do it
  1. 1.Stand on the edge of a step or flat floor, barefoot. Hands lightly on a wall for balance.
  2. 2.Rise up onto your toes slowly (2 sec up), hold briefly at the top.
  3. 3.Lower slowly (3 sec down). Full range of motion matters more than speed.
💡 Critical for your foot twinges. Eccentric loading (the slow lowering) directly strengthens the Achilles and plantar fascia. Do these barefoot, always.
Watch demo on YouTube
Coach note: These 6 exercises will feel easy at first. They're supposed to — you're building connective tissue, not just muscle. Around week 3 you'll start feeling your glutes actually fire when you run. That's the adaptation happening.
Workout guide
💪 Strength B
Upper body & push/pull — do this every Friday
⏱ 30 min 🔁 3 rounds 📍 Needs pull-up bar
Structure:  3 rounds · 30–45 sec rest between exercises · 90 sec rest between rounds
01 Push-Up
10–15 reps
How to do it
  1. 1.Hands slightly wider than shoulder-width, body in a straight line from head to heels.
  2. 2.Lower your chest to the floor with control (2 sec down). Elbows at about 45° from your body — not flared wide.
  3. 3.Press back up fully. Don't let your hips sag.
💡 If full push-ups are hard: Elevate hands on a counter or bench. Never do knee push-ups — they eliminate core demand. Elevated hands keep the full movement pattern.
Watch demo on YouTube
02 Pull-Up / Negative Pull-Up
5–8 reps
How to do it
  1. 1.Hang from the bar, hands shoulder-width apart, palms facing away (overhand grip).
  2. 2.Squeeze your shoulder blades together, then pull your chin above the bar.
  3. 3.Lower slowly (3 sec) back to full hang. Don't kip or swing.
💡 If you can't do pull-ups yet: Do negatives — jump or step to the top position, then lower yourself over 5 seconds. This is how you build to full pull-ups. Your swimming background means this will come back fast.
Watch demo on YouTube
03 Pike Push-Up
8 reps
How to do it
  1. 1.Start in a push-up position. Walk your feet forward and pike your hips high so your body forms an inverted V.
  2. 2.Bend your elbows and lower the top of your head toward the floor between your hands.
  3. 3.Press back up to the V position. Keep your hips high throughout.
💡 Targets the front deltoids and triceps with a shoulder-press movement pattern — no equipment needed. Builds shoulder stability important for your rotator cuff history.
Watch demo on YouTube
04 Superman Hold
10 reps · 2-sec hold
How to do it
  1. 1.Lie face down, arms extended overhead, legs straight.
  2. 2.Simultaneously lift your arms, chest, and legs off the floor by squeezing your glutes and back muscles.
  3. 3.Hold 2 seconds at the top, then lower with control. Don't jerk or bounce.
💡 This is your posterior chain strengthener — erector spinae, glutes, upper back. Critical for desk workers and runners. Directly addresses the lower back fatigue you may feel in early weeks.
Watch demo on YouTube
05 Chair Dip
10 reps
How to do it
  1. 1.Sit on the edge of a sturdy chair, hands gripping the front edge, fingers forward. Slide your hips off the seat.
  2. 2.Lower yourself by bending your elbows until they reach 90°. Keep your back close to the chair.
  3. 3.Press back up to straight arms. Don't shrug your shoulders — keep them down and back.
💡 Use a sturdy wooden chair or a low bench. The closer your feet are to you, the easier it is. Extend legs straight for more challenge.
Watch demo on YouTube
06 Mountain Climbers
20 reps (10 each side)
How to do it
  1. 1.Start in a high push-up position. Wrists under shoulders, body in a plank.
  2. 2.Drive your right knee toward your chest, then return it and switch to the left. Alternate at a controlled pace.
  3. 3.Keep hips level — don't let them bounce up and down. Core stays tight throughout.
💡 This finishes the workout with your heart rate elevated. Controlled tempo (not a sprint) keeps core engagement high. Think of it as running in place with full core involvement.
Watch demo on YouTube
Coach note: Strength B complements your running — upper body stability makes you a significantly more efficient runner. The pull-up work will come back fast given your swimming background. Don't be surprised if you're stronger than you think on day one.

🧘 Mobility Routine
Full-body flexibility & joint health — do this after long runs and on Sunday
⏱ 15–20 min 📍 Barefoot when possible 🔁 Do in order
Do this after runs or as a standalone session.  No rounds — just flow through each movement once in order. Barefoot on a mat or carpet.
01 Toe Curls & Arch Raises
2 × 15 reps
How to do it
  1. 1.Stand barefoot on a hard floor (not carpet). Spread your toes wide, then curl them under like you're gripping the floor.
  2. 2.For arch raises: keep toes on the floor, try to lift the arch of your foot without curling your toes. Hold 2 sec at the top.
  3. 3.Progress: try picking up a small towel or marbles with your toes.
💡 This is the most direct fix for your foot twinges. These tiny muscles are almost never trained but do enormous work when running. 2 weeks of this consistently and you'll feel a difference.
Watch demo on YouTube
02 Ankle Circles & Alphabet
30 sec each foot
How to do it
  1. 1.Sit or stand on one foot. Lift the other foot off the floor.
  2. 2.Draw large, slow circles with your ankle — 10 clockwise, 10 counterclockwise.
  3. 3.Then trace the letters A through Z with your big toe. Go slowly and make the letters big.
💡 Sounds silly, works extremely well. The alphabet forces full ankle range in all directions. Often neglected and very important as mileage builds.
Watch demo on YouTube
03 Kneeling Hip Flexor Stretch
45 sec each side
How to do it
  1. 1.Kneel on one knee (use a folded towel for cushion). Other foot forward, knee bent at 90°.
  2. 2.Squeeze the glute of your back leg and gently push your hips forward until you feel a stretch in the front of the back hip.
  3. 3.Hold the stretch. Don't lean forward from the waist — stay tall and upright. Breathe through it.
💡 This will be very tight at first after years of desk work. That's expected and fine. The hip flexor tightness is directly linked to the lower back fatigue many runners feel. Be patient — it releases by week 4.
Watch demo on YouTube
04 World's Greatest Stretch
5 reps each side
How to do it
  1. 1.Step your right foot forward into a deep lunge. Left knee stays off the ground.
  2. 2.Place your right hand on the floor beside your right foot. Rotate your left arm up toward the ceiling, following it with your eyes.
  3. 3.Hold 2 seconds at the top, return hand to floor, then shift hips back to stretch the hamstring. That's one rep. Switch sides.
💡 The single best mobility exercise for runners. Hits hip flexors, thoracic rotation, hamstrings, and ankle dorsiflexion in one movement. Watch it on YouTube once to lock in the pattern — it's worth 30 seconds of your time.
Watch demo on YouTube ← Watch this one first
05 Pigeon Pose
60 sec each side
How to do it
  1. 1.From a push-up position, bring your right knee forward and place it behind your right wrist, shin angled across your body. Left leg extends straight back.
  2. 2.Lower your hips toward the floor. If your hip doesn't reach the floor, place a folded blanket under it for support.
  3. 3.Hold for 60 seconds per side. Breathe slowly and let the tension release — don't fight it.
💡 Deep glute and hip external rotator stretch — the deepest in this routine. Protects your IT band and lower back. The tighter you are here, the more you need it. 60 seconds is not negotiable.
Watch demo on YouTube
06 Calf + Achilles Stretch
45 sec each · straight + bent knee
How to do it
  1. 1.Straight-knee version: Step one foot back, heel flat on the floor. Lean hands into a wall. You should feel the stretch in the belly of the calf. Hold 45 sec.
  2. 2.Bent-knee version: Same position, but bend the back knee slightly. This shifts the stretch lower to the Achilles and soleus. Hold 45 sec.
  3. 3.Switch legs and repeat both.
💡 Both versions are essential — they target different muscles. The bent-knee stretch for the Achilles is the one most runners skip and most need. Do both, every time.
Watch demo on YouTube
07 Thoracic Rotation
10 reps each side
How to do it
  1. 1.Lie on your side with knees stacked and bent at 90°. Bottom arm extended straight, top arm on top of it.
  2. 2.Keeping your knees stacked (don't let them rotate), slowly rotate your top arm open to the floor behind you, following your hand with your eyes.
  3. 3.Hold 2 seconds at the fully open position, then return. Do 10 reps, then switch sides.
💡 Opens the upper back and ribcage — directly improves running posture, breathing efficiency, and shoulder blade mobility. Tall runners often have poor thoracic rotation. This will feel stiff at first and then wonderful by week 3.
Watch demo on YouTube
Coach note: This routine will feel like work for the first 2 weeks. By week 4 it will feel good. By week 8 you'll notice your foot twinges have largely disappeared and your posture has changed. This is the most underrated part of the whole program — most runners skip it and most injuries come from skipping it.

🏃 Pre/Post-Run Routine
Prep before you run, recover after — built to prevent niggles as mileage climbs
⏱ 5 min before · 8 min after 📍 No equipment needed 🛡 Injury prevention focus
The rule:  dynamic movement before you run, static stretching after. Doing it backwards (static stretching cold muscles before running) doesn't help and can mildly increase injury risk.
Why dynamic, not static: Cold static stretching (holding a stretch before you've moved) can temporarily reduce muscle power output. Dynamic movement raises tissue temperature, increases blood flow, and rehearses the running motion. Do this right after your warmup walk, right before you pick up the pace.
01 Leg Swings (front to back)
10 each leg
How to do it
  1. 1.Stand next to a wall or tree for light balance support.
  2. 2.Swing one leg forward and backward in a controlled pendulum motion, keeping your torso upright.
  3. 3.Let the range of motion grow naturally over the 10 reps — don't force it on rep 1.
💡 Loosens hip flexors and hamstrings dynamically — exactly the muscles that do the most work in your stride.
Watch demo on YouTube
02 Leg Swings (side to side)
10 each leg
How to do it
  1. 1.Facing the wall this time, swing one leg side to side across your body, like a pendulum.
  2. 2.Keep your standing leg slightly bent and your core engaged for balance.
  3. 3.Controlled motion — this isn't about speed or maximum range.
💡 Targets adductors and abductors (inner/outer thigh) and glute medius — small stabilizer muscles that quietly prevent knee and hip pain as mileage builds.
Watch demo on YouTube
03 Walking Knee Hugs
8 each leg, walking
How to do it
  1. 1.Walking forward, lift one knee up toward your chest and hug it briefly with both arms.
  2. 2.Release, step forward, and repeat with the other leg as you continue walking.
  3. 3.Keep your standing leg's knee soft, not locked straight, during the hug.
💡 Dynamic glute and hip flexor stretch combined with a balance challenge — mimics the high-knee phase of your running stride.
Watch demo on YouTube
04 Walking Lunges with Twist
6 each leg, walking
How to do it
  1. 1.Step forward into a lunge, back knee hovering just above the floor.
  2. 2.From the bottom of the lunge, rotate your torso toward your front leg, then back to center.
  3. 3.Push through your front foot to step forward into the next lunge on the other side. Continue walking forward.
💡 Combines a hip flexor stretch with thoracic rotation — covers two problem areas for tall, desk-bound runners in one fluid movement.
Watch demo on YouTube
05 High Knees
20 sec
How to do it
  1. 1.Jog in place or move slowly forward, driving your knees up toward hip height.
  2. 2.Stay light on your feet — quick ground contact, not a hard stomp.
  3. 3.Pump your arms naturally as if you were actually running.
💡 Raises heart rate and core temperature right before you start — bridges the gap between walking warmup and actual running pace.
Watch demo on YouTube
06 Butt Kicks
20 sec
How to do it
  1. 1.Jog in place or move slowly forward, kicking your heels back to touch (or come close to) your glutes.
  2. 2.Keep your torso upright and stay light on your feet.
  3. 3.This is your last dynamic move — right after this, head into your easy warmup pace.
💡 Dynamically loosens the quads through full range of motion. Pairs naturally with high knees as the final activation before you run.
Watch demo on YouTube
Coach note: 5 minutes, every single run, no exceptions — even on easy days when you're tempted to skip straight out the door. This is the routine most likely to prevent the small niggles (shin splints, IT band irritation, calf tightness) that derail people right around the mileage increases in Phase 2 and 3.
Why static, not dynamic: After running, your muscles are warm and pliable — the ideal state for static stretching to actually improve range of motion and aid recovery. Do this within 10–15 minutes of finishing, before you've cooled down and showered.
01 Standing Quad Stretch
30 sec each leg
How to do it
  1. 1.Stand on one leg (hold a wall for balance if needed). Grab your other ankle behind you.
  2. 2.Gently pull your heel toward your glute, keeping your knees close together and hips pushed slightly forward.
  3. 3.Hold without bouncing. Breathe normally — don't hold your breath against the stretch.
💡 Quads absorb a huge amount of impact on long runs and downhills. This is one of the first muscles to tighten up post-run.
Watch demo on YouTube
02 Standing Hamstring Stretch
30 sec each leg
How to do it
  1. 1.Place one heel on a low step, curb, or stair, leg straight (or place foot flat on the ground, slight forward stride).
  2. 2.Keeping your back flat, hinge forward from your hips until you feel a stretch behind the thigh.
  3. 3.Hold. Don't round your lower back to go deeper — that loses the hamstring stretch and stresses your spine instead.
💡 Tight hamstrings are one of the most common contributors to lower back tightness in runners. Hinge from the hip, not the spine.
Watch demo on YouTube
03 Standing IT Band / Glute Stretch
30 sec each side
How to do it
  1. 1.Cross one leg behind the other (like crossing your ankles while standing).
  2. 2.Keeping both legs mostly straight, hinge at the hips and reach toward your toes, letting your hip drift to one side.
  3. 3.You should feel the stretch along the outside of your hip/thigh on the back leg. Hold, then switch.
💡 The IT band itself doesn't stretch much, but this targets the glute medius and tensor fasciae latae that feed into it — the actual source of most "IT band syndrome" in new runners.
Watch demo on YouTube
04 Calf Stretch (straight + bent knee)
30 sec each, each leg
How to do it
  1. 1.Hands on a wall, step one foot back with the heel flat on the floor and leg straight. Lean forward gently.
  2. 2.Hold 30 sec, feeling the stretch in the upper calf (gastrocnemius).
  3. 3.Bend the back knee slightly, keeping the heel down, to shift the stretch lower into the Achilles/soleus. Hold 30 sec, then switch legs.
💡 Same move as in your mobility routine — it's worth repeating post-run specifically because calves and Achilles take on more load as your mileage and pace increase. Don't skip the bent-knee version.
Watch demo on YouTube
05 Figure-4 Hip Stretch (lying down)
30–45 sec each side
How to do it
  1. 1.Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat on the floor.
  2. 2.Cross your right ankle over your left knee, shin forming a "4" shape.
  3. 3.Reach through and gently pull your left thigh toward your chest until you feel a stretch in the right glute/hip. Hold, then switch.
💡 A gentler, lying-down alternative to pigeon pose — good for a quick post-run floor stretch when you're short on time or it's a lighter mileage day.
Watch demo on YouTube
06 Child's Pose (low back release)
45–60 sec
How to do it
  1. 1.Kneel on the floor, big toes touching, knees spread wide.
  2. 2.Sit your hips back toward your heels and walk your hands forward, lowering your chest toward the floor.
  3. 3.Let your forehead rest on the floor or a cushion. Breathe deeply into your lower back for the full hold.
💡 A passive decompression for the lower back after the repetitive impact of running. Great way to end the stretch sequence and downshift before you go about your day.
Watch demo on YouTube
Coach note: You're not chasing pain right now, which is exactly the right time to build this habit — prevention is far easier than rehab. On long run days, this sequence plus your full mobility routine (see the Mobility tab) is the one-two punch that keeps you healthy as Phase 2 and 3 mileage climbs.
